Overview

Plagued by the abundance of crowdfunding websites, I wanted to create a virtual stage to give underground internet content creators and their fans a place to go.

The Challenge

Originally, Booster was going to be a crowdfunding website for the entertainment industry but the competition was polluted. The trouble was how could I spin this and make it different.

The Answer

In a game show fashion the user will decide the creator’s fate. Updated monthly, Booster will review the newest pool of talent from platforms such as Youtube, Snapchat, Twitch, and film. Booster will then place them on the front page for user’s to vote through in an upvote and downvote system. If the creators get enough votes by the end of the month they will get rewarded. The prizes will range from new investors, cash, or even up to 1000 new fans.

I started my research by targeting who our main audience would be. By sending out surveys for both content creators and the potential users of Booster.

Important things were noted. Booster would end up being used primarily for entertainment purposes while also keeping a crowdfunding backbone. According to the survey results, I dropped a lot of features that I originally had planned.

Competitive Analysis

Kickstarter is for entertainers and generally supports new and unique ideas. Gumroad is not much of a competitor but would be selling things to a certain viewer kind of like Booster. Social sharing gives Gumroad a unique advantage to stand out.

Patreon is the main competitor for Booster. They are heavily entertainment driven for all creative types and hit on the same user base that we do. They have a system that works and a solid website.

GoFundMe is a competitor known by their name, not by website design or demographic. They are the most generic crowdfunding site out there. IndieGoGo is great for the underground scene. They could prove to be a big competitor because they appeal to the startup industry, and also very investor friendly.

Personas

By exploring personal experiences using Snapchat, Twitch, and human connections I wanted to showcase creators that would use my platform as a source for new fans and money. Also including one casual fan that is looking for something new and exciting.

User Stories & Flow

Due to time constraints and a limit on website capabilities the user stories had an original flow that got changed later to accomodate this. It stayed true to the core features but also some smaller things got left behind after we further conducted research. I had to leave out analytics, a rewards system, email notifications will play a role when the creators get reviewed.

Mockups / First edition

After these mockups started to take shape, it also shaped how my project would look in the end. But based on time constraints and how far we wanted to go, that helped dictate the overall tone of Booster.

Mockups / Roadblock & Solution

Leaving it all to user testing, I gathered what information I could and re-designed my mockups having a new mindset. Not relying on my guts to iterate a design was worth it in the end. The Booster look finally came about.

Branding

Heavy inspiration came from my mood board that I created. Neon-underground was the original idea but after further testing, it was better off going with a more natural, minimilistic, and clean feel.

The colors changed from neon pinks and purples to lighter blue and red, giving it much more personality. Adding shapes is what gave it character. Learning how to fight with this process and the struggling contrasts, I landed on more greys and whites based on more user research.

I kept the logo design because the underground feeling made sense to me and is where I come from as a person. I like to see things emerge and become successful.

The Finished Project

Hitting some major speedbumps on the way, I finally figured it out. Choosing my color, backgrounds, and layout wouldn’t have been possible without user research and I found out how pivotal that part of designing is.

Conclusion

At one point I thought it was all going to fall apart. Hitting that roadblock of inspiration and having the original look not work, it took a lot to get it right. Bigger projects can’t be rushed. Given the proper amount of time I would have loved to expand on the user settings, an interactive video for the front pages, as well as adding a pop up tutorial that would help users figure out the mechanics. I learned to trust what others had to say, while also steering clear of feedback that may not have helped. I know where my skills are as a designer, I’ve pinpointed a lot of my strengths and weaknesses while also exploring some new territories while building Booster.
